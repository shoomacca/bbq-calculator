How to Use a Meat Thermometer

ESTIMATED READ TIME: 4 MINUTES

A meat thermometer is the single most impactful tool in a BBQ cook's kit. More important than the smoker brand, the wood choice, or the rub recipe. If you are guessing doneness by feel or time alone, you are leaving too much to chance.

Here is everything you need to know about instant-read, leave-in probe, and wireless multi-probe thermometers.

Types of Meat Thermometers

Instant-Read Thermometer

A handheld probe you insert into meat to get a temperature reading in 2–3 seconds. You remove it after each reading.

Best for quick spot checks, checking multiple areas of a large cut, and verifying final doneness before pulling the meat off the heat.

Leave-In Probe Thermometer

A probe attached to a cable that stays in the meat throughout the entire cook. The display (or Bluetooth transmitter) sits outside the smoker.

Best for monitoring the internal temperature of large cuts (brisket, pork shoulder) without opening the lid.

Where to Insert the Probe

Location matters. A poorly placed probe gives a false reading and can result in undercooked or dry meat.

  • Insert into the thickest part of the meat — this is the last part to reach temperature.
  • Avoid bone — bone conducts heat differently than meat and will give a falsely high reading. Keep the probe tip at least 1cm away from any bone.
  • Avoid fat pockets — fat renders at a lower temperature than muscle. Probing into a fat pocket gives a falsely high reading.
  • For brisket — probe the flat/thickest section of the flat, not the point.

How to Calibrate Your Thermometer

Thermometers drift over time. Check calibration before an important cook using the Ice Water Test:

  1. Fill a glass with crushed ice and cold water.
  2. Insert the probe tip into the ice water (not touching the sides or bottom).
  3. Wait 30 seconds. It should read 0°C ± 1°C.

If your thermometer is off by more than 2°C, either calibrate it (most instant-reads have a calibration nut under the dial) or factor in the offset.
